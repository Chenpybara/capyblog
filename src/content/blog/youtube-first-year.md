One year ago, I uploaded my first video. It was a ten-minute ramble about camera gear, recorded on my iPhone, lit by a desk lamp. It got 47 views.

Today, after 52 videos, here's what I've learned.

## The Numbers

Let's get the analytics out of the way:

| Month | Subs | Total Views | Videos Published |
| :---- | :--- | :---------- | :--------------- |
| 1     | 12   | 230         | 4                |
| 3     | 89   | 1,200       | 12               |
| 6     | 340  | 5,800       | 26               |
| 9     | 890  | 18,400      | 40               |
| 12    | 2,400| 62,000      | 52               |

Not viral numbers. But consistent, steady growth.

## What Actually Worked

### Consistency > Quality

I published every week without fail. Some videos were good. Some were terrible. But the algorithm rewards consistency, and so does your own skill development.

### Thumbnails Are Everything

A mediocre video with a great thumbnail outperforms a great video with a mediocre thumbnail. Every single time. I now spend 30 minutes on thumbnails for every video.

### Titles Matter More Than You Think

My highest-performing videos have titles that create curiosity:

- ❌ "Camera Review 2025"
- ✅ "I Switched to This Camera and Everything Changed"

## What Didn't Work

### Chasing Trends

Every time I made a video because it was trending rather than because I cared about the topic, the video underperformed. Authentic interest translates through the camera.

### Overproducing Early Content

I spent two weeks editing my third video. It got fewer views than the one I edited in an afternoon. Ship it, learn, improve.

### Comparing Myself to Established Creators

This is poison. Comparing your month one to someone else's year five is a recipe for quitting. I had to actively unfollow creators who made me feel inadequate.

## Is It Worth It?

Yes. Not for the money (I made roughly $180 this year from YouTube), but for the skills I've built, the people I've connected with, and the confidence I've gained.

If you're on the fence about starting — start. Don't wait until you have the right gear. Don't wait until you're "ready." Just start.

The best time to start was a year ago. The second best time is today.
